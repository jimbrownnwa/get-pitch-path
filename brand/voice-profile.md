# Pitchpath — Voice Profile

> Done-for-you interactive quiz funnel service. We build the quiz, the funnel, and the backend email sequences. The client gets a conversion machine — we do the work.

## Voice Summary

Direct-response operator who has built these funnels for real clients and has the numbers to prove it. Reads like a founder who watched businesses bleed money on static opt-in forms, started building quiz funnels that actually converted, and now does it as a service. Confident without being hype-y. Every sentence earns its place. Numbers over adjectives. Mechanics over theory. Results over features.

This voice should make a skeptical marketer think: "These people have actually shipped this for real businesses. I want them to do it for mine."

## Core Personality Traits

- **Practitioner, not guru:** Talks like someone who has built dozens of quiz funnels and watched them convert — not someone teaching a course about it. "Here is what happened when we built this for a $1.8M course business" beats "here are 7 principles of conversion."
- **Numerically honest:** Uses specific numbers — real ones or honestly-framed estimates. "Lifted opt-ins from 3.1% to 11.4%" beats "skyrocket your conversions." If you cannot back up a number, you do not use the number.
- **Allergic to marketing-speak:** Rejects words like "leverage," "unlock," "empower," "holistic," "seamless." Says the plain version instead. If a real human would not say it at a bar, cut it.
- **Quietly confrontational:** Willing to name the bad thing ("static opt-in forms are dead," "DIY quiz builders got you a 2% opt-in for a reason"). Does not hedge into mush. But also does not yell or shame — the tone is "let me show you what we build" not "you are stupid."
- **Operator energy:** Short, clipped, action-first. Verbs at the front. Fragments are welcome when they earn rhythm.
- **Service confidence:** We do this for a living. The voice never apologizes for being a service, never justifies the price, never compares to DIY tools as if they are the baseline. The baseline is hiring us and getting it done right.

## Tone Spectrum

| Dimension | Position | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Formal ↔ Casual | Casual, but tight (7/10 casual) | Contractions yes. Slang rare. No "hey fam." No corporate padding either. |
| Serious ↔ Playful | 70/30 serious with dry wit | Humor shows up as understatement, never as jokes. No puns. No exclamation points (almost ever). |
| Reserved ↔ Bold | Bold (8/10) | Strong claims, backed by numbers. Willing to call a common tactic broken. |
| Simple ↔ Sophisticated | Simple words, sophisticated ideas | 6th-grade vocabulary carrying senior-marketer thinking. Complexity lives in the idea, not the word. |
| Warm ↔ Direct | Direct (8/10) | Not cold. Not performatively friendly. Respects the reader's time as the first act of warmth. |

## Vocabulary

**Words/phrases to USE:**
- "we build" / "we built" — core service framing, always active voice
- "done for you" — only when naming the model, not as a crutch
- "convert" — primary success verb (not "engage," not "activate")
- "opt-in" — the actual industry noun people search
- "warm" / "cold" traffic — operator vocabulary
- "dead leads" / "cold list" — name the problem plainly
- "here is the thing" — transition into the real point
- "the math" / "do the math" — signature framing for ROI claims
- "interactive experience" — core positioning lever (not "quiz tool")
- "segment" (verb) — core mechanic language
- "the quiz does the qualifying" — signature pattern phrase
- "your funnel" / "your quiz" — it belongs to the client, we build it for them
- "ship" / "shipped" — for delivery ("we shipped this in 10 days")
- "stop" / "quit" / "kill" — confrontational verbs for bad tactics
- "book a call" / "see our work" / "let's talk" — service CTAs
- Specific numbers with units: "3.1%", "11 questions", "$42 CPA", "90 seconds", "10 business days"

**Words/phrases to AVOID:**
- "leverage" — say "use"
- "unlock" — say what actually happens
- "empower" — marketing cliche, means nothing
- "solutions" / "solution" — vague noun, say the actual thing ("quiz funnel," "email sequence")
- "revolutionary" / "game-changing" / "cutting-edge" — hype with no substance
- "seamless" / "frictionless" / "holistic" — corporate filler
- "journey" (customer journey is fine once; never as a metaphor twice)
- "delight" (as a verb) — cringe SaaS energy
- "dive in" / "deep dive" — overused content-marketing tic
- "in today's fast-paced world" — instant trash can
- "we believe that" — if you believe it, just say it
- "platform" / "tool" / "software" — Pitchpath is a service, not a product. Never frame as a tech product.
- "sign up" / "start free trial" / "get started" — SaaS CTAs that don't apply
- "template" / "drag-and-drop" / "no-code" — DIY tool language, opposite of our positioning
- Em-dash chains longer than one per sentence — muddies the rhythm
- Exclamation points — they read as desperate; one every ~500 words, max
- Emoji in body copy — only tolerable in short-form social if at all
- "Simply" / "just" as softeners — both weaken the claim, cut them

**Jargon level:** Moderate. Uses real marketer vocabulary (CPA, opt-in, lead magnet, cold traffic, segment, CTR, AOV) without explaining it — the audience already knows. Skips academic or agency jargon.

**Profanity:** Occasional, only where it lands. One well-placed "bullshit" per long-form piece is fine. Never in headlines. Never aimed at the reader.

## Rhythm & Structure

**Sentences:** Heavy mix. Short punchy lines (3-7 words) do the heavy lifting. Then one medium sentence (12-18 words) explains the mechanic. Then back to short. Almost never long.

Pattern to hit:
> Short sentence. Another short one. Then a slightly longer sentence that earns its length by actually explaining the thing. Back to short.

**Paragraphs:** 1-3 sentences. White space is a feature. A five-line paragraph is a code smell — break it.

**Openings:** Lead with the claim, the number, or the client result. Not the setup. Not "in this article we will explore." The first line should make the reader think "wait, what?" or "okay, prove it."

Good openings:
- "Static opt-in forms convert at 2%. The quiz funnels we build convert at 30%+."
- "Your lead magnet is not broken. Your qualifier is. We fix that."
- "We built a quiz funnel for a course creator last month. Her opt-in rate went from 3.1% to 14.2%. Same traffic."

**Closings:** One clear ask. No "thanks for reading." No sign-offs that hedge. The CTA is service-oriented: "Book the call" / "See our work" / "Let's talk about your funnel." Always pair with a reason or result.

**Formatting:**
- Short headers, often as claims or numbers ("The 2% problem")
- Bullet lists only when the bullets are parallel and scannable
- Bold used sparingly — one or two load-bearing phrases per section
- No italics for emphasis — rely on sentence construction
- Minimal emoji anywhere; zero in body copy

## POV & Address

**First person:** "I" for founder-voice thought leadership. "We" for service/company copy (the team builds it). Default to "we" — Pitchpath is a service, not a solo freelancer brand.

**Reader address:** "You" — direct, constant, second person. Never "folks," "friends," "guys," "marketers." Just "you."

**Relationship stance:** Expert-to-busy-operator. The reader is smart, understands marketing, but does not have time (or desire) to build a quiz funnel themselves. We are the people who do this all day. The tone is "we handle this, you handle your business" — never condescending, never begging for the sale.

## Service-Specific Voice Rules

1. **Never justify the price.** The voice assumes done-for-you is worth it. If the reader wants DIY, there are tools for that. We are not competing with Typeform.
2. **Position against DIY, not against other services.** The enemy is the half-built quiz someone made in Interact at 2am, not another agency. "You could build this yourself. You could also do your own taxes."
3. **Results over process.** The reader does not care that we use [tool X] or follow [methodology Y]. They care that the quiz funnel we build will convert. Lead with the outcome.
4. **Specificity signals expertise.** "We built an 11-question branching quiz with 4 result paths and a 7-email nurture sequence" beats "we create custom quiz funnels." The detail proves we actually do this.
5. **Delivery language matters.** Use "build," "ship," "deliver," "launch" — active verbs that imply a thing getting done. Never "onboard," "implement," "deploy" — consultant-speak.

| Sales Email | Warmer, founder voice. Story-driven — "we just shipped this for [client type]." One CTA per email. | Short paragraphs. One idea per email. CTA at the bottom = reply or book link. Subject lines ≤ 50 chars, curiosity or number-forward. | 120-250 words body. |
| Ads (Meta/Google) | Punchiest. Most confrontational. Named problem in the first 5 words. DIY vs. done-for-you contrast. | Hook + agitate + proof (client result) + CTA. No setup. No "introducing." | Primary text: 80-150 chars. Headlines: ≤ 40 chars. |
| LinkedIn | Operator-expertise forward. Case-study snippets. "We just built this" energy. | Line-break-heavy, one thought per line. Numbers up top. End with an insight, not a pitch. | 120-250 words. |
| Twitter/X | Sharpest. Most opinionated. Willing to challenge "build your own funnel" conventional wisdom. | One idea per post. Threads for case study breakdowns. Numbers in the hook. | ≤ 260 chars per post. Threads 5-9 posts. |
| Blog / SEO | More thorough, still voiced. Case studies and teardowns. The voice does not disappear because it is longer. | H2s as claims, not keywords. Intro ≤ 80 words. Sub-sections ≤ 200 words. | 1,200-2,200 words. |
| Case Study | Most detailed. Walk through what we built, why, and what happened. Numbers are the star. | Before/after structure. Problem → what we built → results. Include specific funnel architecture details. | 800-1,500 words. |

## Signature Patterns (use these as templates)

1. **The client-result lead.** Open with a specific client result. Then say what we built. Then say why it worked.
   > "We built a quiz funnel for a course creator last month. Opt-ins went from 3.1% to 14.2%. Same ad spend. Here is what we did."

2. **The "here is the thing" pivot.** Mid-piece, use "Here is the thing:" to pivot from setup to the real insight. One per piece, max. It is a load-bearing phrase — do not burn it.

3. **The DIY teardown.** Name the DIY approach. Show why it underperforms. Offer the done-for-you alternative in the next sentence.
   > "You could build a quiz in Typeform this weekend. You will get a form that looks like a quiz but converts like a form. We build quiz funnels that do the selling — the quiz, the branching logic, the follow-up emails, the segmented pitches. That is why our clients convert at 5-10x."

4. **The mechanics-over-magic close.** Never "transform your business." Always "here is the exact thing we build." The CTA references the deliverable, not the dream: "Book a call and we will walk you through a quiz funnel we built for a business like yours."

5. **The short-sentence hammer.** When a claim is the most important line on the page, give it its own paragraph and cap it at 6 words. Make the reader's eye land on it alone.

## Example Phrases

**On-brand:**
- "Static opt-in forms are dead. The quiz funnels we build convert 5-10x better. Here is why, with numbers."
- "You don't need more traffic. You need a funnel that qualifies the traffic you already have. We build that."
- "We shipped a full quiz funnel for a $1.8M course business last quarter. Opt-ins went from 3.1% to 11.4%. Same ad spend."
- "Stop renting attention. Start capturing intent. We handle the build."
- "The quiz does the qualifying so your sales calls stop being intake calls."
- "You could build this yourself. You could also do your own taxes."
- "We build the quiz funnel, the email sequences, and the segmented follow-up. You get customers."

**Off-brand (do not write like this):**
- "Unlock the power of interactive experiences to delight your audience." — every word is dead
- "Our revolutionary quiz funnel solution empowers brands to seamlessly convert visitors into customers." — marketing-speak on marketing-speak
- "Sign up for our platform and start building your quiz today." — we are not a SaaS, there is no sign-up
- "We're so excited to share our amazing new service with you!" — hype without substance
- "Choose from hundreds of templates to create your perfect quiz." — DIY tool language, the opposite of what we do
- "Our team will implement a customized interactive engagement solution for your brand." — consultant-speak, strip it all

## Do's and Don'ts

**DO:**
- Lead with a client result or a bold claim in every piece
- Use short sentences as hammers, longer sentences as explanations
- Name the broken thing (DIY quizzes, static forms) before offering the fix (what we build)
- Use "you" relentlessly
- Reference specific deliverables: "11-question branching quiz," "7-email nurture sequence," "4 result paths"
- Say the unglamorous true version of the sentence
- Cut the first sentence of every draft — it is almost always warm-up
- Always end with a service CTA: book a call, see our work, reply to this email

**DON'T:**
- Use SaaS language: "platform," "tool," "sign up," "free trial," "get started," "template"
- Use consultant-speak: "implement," "onboard," "deploy," "solution," "stakeholders"
- Use "leverage," "unlock," "empower," "seamless," "holistic," "revolutionary," or "delight"
- Open with "In today's..." or "Did you know...?"
- Write a paragraph longer than 4 lines on mobile
- Use emoji in body copy
- Compare Pitchpath to other quiz tools as if they are direct competitors — they are DIY, we are done-for-you
- Justify the price or apologize for being a service
- Hedge with "simply," "just," "maybe," "I think" in headlines or CTAs
- Write anything you would be embarrassed to read out loud to a skeptical operator friend
